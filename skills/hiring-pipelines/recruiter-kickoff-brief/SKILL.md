# Recruiter Kickoff Brief

Give a recruiter the insider context a job description leaves out — what the role really is, who succeeds, who looks good but won't, and how to pitch it — so they source and sell the right people.

## Inputs to gather

Gather these before writing. If any are missing, ask for them in a single batched question — never invent company metrics, comp, or stack details. Mark anything genuinely unavailable as **Unknown** in the output.

- **Role** — title, level, and team
- **Team context** — size, stage, what the team builds, tech stack, company metrics (ARR, headcount, scale)
- **Urgency / timeline** — how fast the role must close and why
- **Process** — interview stages and target timeline, if defined

## Steps

1. Translate the role into **what this person actually does day-to-day** — concrete first project and real responsibilities, not JD language. Name what kind of work it is NOT (e.g. "not a CRUD role").
2. Define the **ideal candidate profile**: skills, experience patterns, and traits that predict success here. Be specific about years, domains, and instincts (e.g. "immediately asks 'what happens when this fails?'"). Note where adjacent backgrounds are acceptable.
3. List **anti-patterns** — candidates who look good on paper but won't succeed here, each with the reason (wrong build-vs-integrate mindset, no startup experience, missing a non-negotiable concept).
4. Write **3-5 selling points** as phrases the recruiter can use in outreach — impact, scope, greenfield work, stability, team shape, genuinely interesting problems.
5. Anticipate **common objections** and give the rebuttal for each (stack mismatch, company-stage risk, missing domain experience). Use real metrics where available; mark unverified ones as such.
6. Give **sourcing guidance**: target companies (and why those — they teach the right instincts), keywords, and channels.
7. Summarize the **process**: ordered interview stages with durations and a target time from first screen to offer.
8. Adapt to context: for an **external recruiter**, sharpen selling points and anti-patterns (they pattern-match on what you tell them); for a **hard-to-fill role**, add a creative-sourcing angle (adjacent roles, passive candidates); for **multiple open roles**, give the recruiter a priority ranking; recommend a **weekly 15-minute sync** to feed back fast, specific signal.
9. Assemble the output in the format below.

## Output format

```
**Recruiter Brief: [Role, Team]**

**What this person actually does**
[Concrete day-to-day, first project, and what it is NOT.]

**Ideal Candidate**
- [Skill / experience pattern / trait]

**Anti-Patterns (skip these candidates)**
- [Profile] — [why they won't succeed here]

**Selling Points (use these in outreach)**
1. [Quotable phrase or fact]

**Common Objections**
- "[objection]" → "[rebuttal, with metric if available]"

**Where to Source**
- Companies: [targets and why]
- Keywords: [search terms]
- Channels: [where to look]

**Process**
1. [Stage] ([duration]) → 2. [Stage] → ... → Offer
Target: [timeline from first screen to offer].
```

## Boundaries

- Never fabricate company metrics (ARR, NRR, customer counts), comp, or stack — mark unknowns as **Unknown**, and flag candidate-volunteered figures as unverified.
- Never frame anti-patterns around protected characteristics; base them only on role-relevant skills, mindset, and experience.
- Never overstate stability or upside to a recruiter; they will repeat it to candidates verbatim.
- Never invent interview stages or timelines the team hasn't agreed to.

## Chaining

- After this, offer **phone-screen-script** to prepare the hiring-manager screen for candidates the recruiter sources.
- If the role and priority aren't settled yet, run **hiring-plan** first.
