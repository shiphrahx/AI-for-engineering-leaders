# Manager README

Give direct reports a head start on working with you — surfacing your working style, priorities, and limitations honestly so nobody has to learn them by trial and error. First-person, direct, a working document — not a PR exercise or a mission statement.

## Inputs to gather

Gather these before drafting. If any are missing, ask in a single batched question — never invent the manager's preferences, struggles, or non-negotiables. Mark anything genuinely unavailable as **Unknown**.

- **How I work** — scheduling, communication style, response times, async vs sync
- **What I care about** — 3-4 things you consistently prioritise (honest, not aspirational)
- **How to get the best from me** — context, framing, or heads-up that helps
- **How I give feedback** — frequency, format, directness
- **What I struggle with** — 1-2 genuine limitations or tendencies
- **Things I probably won't change** — non-negotiable working preferences

## Steps

1. Draft **How I work** — concrete scheduling, communication channels, and response-time norms.
2. Draft **What I care about** — 3-4 genuine priorities, stated honestly; cut anything aspirational that you don't actually live.
3. Draft **How to get the best from me** — the context and framing that makes you useful to them.
4. Draft **How I give feedback** — your real cadence, format, and directness, including any caveat (e.g. "I notice problems before I notice wins").
5. Draft **What I struggle with** — 1-2 honest limitations, framed so reports know how to work around them.
6. Draft **Things I probably won't change** — the non-negotiables, stated upfront so they're not a surprise.
7. Keep the tone first-person, direct, and honest. Avoid mission-statement language. Adapt to context: for a **new team**, share it before or in the first 1:1 cycle and invite them to write their own. For **honest revision**, note this should be revisited every 6-12 months — an outdated README is worse than none. For a **short version**, keep just "How I work", "What I care about", and "What I struggle with" — those carry the most signal.
8. Assemble the output in the format below.

## Output format

```
**Working with [Name] — a README**

**How I work**
[Scheduling, channels, response times, async vs sync]

**What I care about**
[3-4 honest priorities]

**How to get the best from me**
[Context/framing/heads-up that helps]

**How I give feedback**
[Cadence, format, directness, honest caveats]

**What I struggle with**
[1-2 genuine limitations and how to work around them]

**Things I won't change**
[Non-negotiables, stated plainly]
```

## Boundaries

- Never invent the manager's preferences, priorities, struggles, or non-negotiables — these must be the manager's own honest input. Mark unknowns as **Unknown** and ask.
- Don't write aspirational claims dressed as facts; an inaccurate README erodes trust faster than no README.
- Keep it a working doc, not marketing — avoid mission-statement or corporate-values language.
- Don't let "things I won't change" encode preferences that disadvantage reports (e.g. always-on expectations); frame around sustainable, fair norms.
