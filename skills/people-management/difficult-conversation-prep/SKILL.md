# Difficult Conversation Prep

Structure talking points so the conversation is clear, kind, and specific — the person leaves understanding exactly what the issue is, why it matters, and what "better" looks like, without feeling attacked.

## Inputs to gather

Gather these before drafting. If any are missing, ask in a single batched question — never invent the examples, the evidence, or what was said. Mark anything genuinely unavailable as **Unknown**.

- **Person** — name and role
- **What the conversation is about** — the specific issue
- **Specific examples** — dated, observable evidence
- **Desired outcome** — what you want to change
- **Relationship context** — your relationship and how they respond to feedback

## Steps

1. Write an **Opening Frame** — how to start: set the tone and state the purpose. Frame it as "your impact isn't matching your intent", not "you're doing something wrong".
2. Write the **Core Message** — the specific issue stated directly with dated, observable examples. No sandwich method, no interpretations ("you're rude") — just what happened and the effect.
3. Write the **Impact Statement** — why it matters to the team, the project, and their own career.
4. Write a **Listen** prompt — genuine, open questions inviting their perspective, with a note to actually pause and hear them out before moving to solutions; they may have context you don't.
5. Write the **Path Forward** — specific, observable expectations for what changes.
6. Write the **Support Offer** — concrete ways you'll help (calibration, a model peer to learn from, pairing).
7. Write the **Close** — reaffirm the relationship and check how they're feeling; this comes from respect.
8. Adapt to context: if the person **gets defensive**, don't argue — restate the facts and that you're asking for an adjustment, not agreement with the interpretation. For a **missed-promotion conversation**, lead with empathy, be specific about what was missing, and pivot to a clear path forward. For a **conflict between two reports**, talk to each individually first; don't open with a joint mediation. For the **manager's own nervousness**, rehearse the opening sentence out loud. Always **follow up in writing within 24 hours** ("here's what we agreed to") to create accountability.
9. Assemble the output in the format below, with brief parenthetical coaching notes on delivery.

## Output format

```
**Conversation Prep: [Name] — [Topic]**

**Opening Frame**
"[How to start — tone and purpose]"
*(Delivery note)*

**Core Message**
"[The specific issue, with dated examples and observed impact]"
*(Delivery note)*

**Impact Statement**
"[Why it matters — team, project, their career]"

**Listen**
"[Open question inviting their perspective]"
*(Pause and actually listen)*

**Path Forward**
"[Specific, observable expectations]"

**Support Offer**
"[Concrete help you'll provide]"

**Close**
"[Reaffirm the relationship; check how they're feeling]"
```

## Boundaries

- Never invent examples, evidence, quotes, or what was said. Mark unknowns as **Unknown** and ask — vague feedback without specifics is the opposite of the goal.
- State observable behaviour and impact, never character judgments ("you're lazy/rude/difficult"). Keep it about what happened and what changes.
- Base the conversation on documented evidence, not impressions; ensure the standard is one applied to peers, free of bias.
- For sensitive or potentially employment-affecting topics (a behavioural pattern heading toward a PIP, or anything legal), involve HR — and never use this to deliver a predetermined decision dressed as a discussion.
- Keep prep notes confidential; have the conversation in person or video, never by message, and never repeat one report's words to another.

## Chaining

- If the issue is a documented performance pattern that coaching hasn't fixed, offer **pip-documentation** (with HR).
- After the conversation, capture the agreed path forward in the next **performance-review-draft** or follow up in **one-on-one-prep**.
