# One-on-One Prep

Build an agenda personalised to this person's situation — mixing tactical check-ins with career development — so the 1:1 feels like a conversation, not an interrogation. Give them the floor first.

## Inputs to gather

Gather these before drafting. If any are missing, ask for them in a single batched question — never invent the person's goals, recent events, or what they said last time. Mark anything genuinely unavailable as **Unknown**.

- **Person** — name, role, level, tenure
- **Current projects** — what they're working on now
- **Recent context** — retro behaviour, recent wins or struggles, things they said
- **Development goals** — where they want to grow
- **Last 1:1 notes** — open threads and commitments from last time

## Steps

1. Read the context and pick out the specific, recent details — these are what make the agenda feel personal rather than generic.
2. Write an **Opening** that proves you remember their work — a specific question, never "how are you?".
3. Add a **Their Topics** prompt that gives them the floor before your agenda — it's their meeting.
4. Write 1-2 **Project Check-in** questions focused on unblocking them, following up on commitments from the last 1:1 (continuity builds trust).
5. Write one **Growth Topic** question tied to a concrete recent observation and their stated goal — not an abstraction. Ask, don't pronounce: questions invite a conversation, statements close it.
6. Write one **Team Check-in** question about team dynamics or how they're feeling — direct but not pushy; open the door without forcing it.
7. List **FYIs** — upcoming things they should know about.
8. Adapt to context: for a **new report (first 90 days)**, focus on onboarding experience, early blockers, and relationship building — career talk can wait. For a **high performer**, skip tactical check-ins and spend the time on growth, stretch opportunities, and engagement. For **performance concerns**, add a specific, evidence-based feedback item and deliver it now while it's actionable — don't save it for the review. For a **remote 1:1**, add an explicit energy check; remote reports more easily mask burnout.
9. Assemble the output in the format below, with brief parenthetical notes on why a question is framed a certain way.

## Output format

```
**1:1 Prep — [Name] ([Day])**

**Opening**
"[Specific question tied to their current work]"
*(Why this question)*

**Their Topics**
"[Floor-giving question]"

**Project Check-in**
- [Project]: "[Unblocking question]"

**Growth Topic**
"[Developmental question tied to their goal + a specific recent observation]"

**Team Check-in**
"[Question about team dynamics or energy]"
*(How to ask without pushing)*

**FYIs**
- [Upcoming thing they should know]
```

## Boundaries

- Never invent the person's goals, mood, recent events, or quotes. Mark unknowns as **Unknown** and ask.
- Questions are starting points, not a script — the user reads the room and adapts in the moment.
- Don't manufacture a growth or feedback topic to fill the template; if there's nothing real, leave more space for their topics instead.
- These are private conversations. Keep prep notes confidential and never repeat what one report says to another.
- Use judgment on sensitive timing — the user decides what's right for this person today.

## Chaining

- After several 1:1s build a picture, offer **performance-review-draft** to consolidate observations into a review.
- For a hard feedback or conflict topic that needs more structure than a 1:1, offer **difficult-conversation-prep**.
- This is usually a recurring, terminal artifact — no successor is required.
