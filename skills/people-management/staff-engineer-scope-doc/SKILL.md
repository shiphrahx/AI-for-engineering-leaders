# Staff Engineer Scope Document

Clarify a staff engineer's impact — what they own, what they decide, where they lead vs support — for them, for you, and for the org. Specific to this person, this team, this moment; never generic "technical leadership" language.

## Inputs to gather

Gather these before drafting. If any are missing, ask in a single batched question — never invent the area, relationships, or the engineer's strengths. Mark anything genuinely unavailable as **Unknown**.

- **Engineer** — name (or "Staff Engineer")
- **Area of ownership** — e.g. data infrastructure, frontend platform, payments, DevEx
- **Team structure** — who they work with, reporting lines, direct reports (usually none)
- **Current biggest challenge** in their area
- **Known strengths**
- **Known growth areas**

## Steps

1. Write **Role context** — where they sit, the area they cover, and the level of autonomy.
2. Write **Primary responsibilities** — ongoing accountabilities they own end-to-end, not a task list.
3. Write **Decision authority** in three buckets: what they can decide unilaterally, what they must consult on (and with whom), and what must escalate (and to whom).
4. Write **How success is measured** — 3-4 concrete indicators over a 6-12 month horizon tied to the area's actual challenge (e.g. CI p95 build time, time-to-first-PR) — not velocity or output metrics.
5. Write **Working relationships** — who they partner with regularly, the cadence, and in what capacity.
6. Write **What this role is not** — the common scope creep and misunderstandings to head off (not a general-purpose senior to pull into feature work; leads initiatives rather than executing all of them; does not manage people).
7. Keep it to 1-2 pages and specific. Adapt to context: for a **newly promoted staff engineer**, add a 30/60/90-day ramp section before responsibilities. For a **staff engineer without a clear ownership area**, use the draft as the basis for a conversation — the gaps reveal the ambiguity. For the **engineer to write themselves**, frame it as a proposal they draft and you refine together for buy-in.
8. Assemble the output in the format below.

## Output format

```
**Staff Engineer Scope — [Area]**
*[Name] | Updated: [Date]*

---

**Role context**
[Where they sit, the area, the level of autonomy]

---

**Primary responsibilities**
- [Ongoing accountability owned end-to-end]

---

**Decision authority**

*Can decide unilaterally:*
- [Decision]

*Consult [role]:*
- [Decision]

*Escalate to [role]:*
- [Decision]

---

**How success is measured**
By [Date+12 months], success looks like:
- [Concrete outcome indicator, not a velocity metric]

---

**Working relationships**
- **[Partner]:** [Cadence and capacity]

---

**What this role is not**
- [Scope creep or misunderstanding to head off]
```

## Boundaries

- Never invent the area, relationships, challenge, strengths, or growth areas. Mark unknowns as **Unknown** and ask.
- Avoid generic "technical leadership" filler — every line is specific to this person, area, and moment, or it doesn't belong.
- Success measures must be outcome-based over 6-12 months, never velocity or output counts that misframe a staff IC's leverage.
- This describes a role, not a verdict on the person; for performance or readiness judgments use performance-review-draft or promotion-case. Keep any draft referencing a named individual confidential until shared with them.

## Chaining

- No natural successor — this is usually a terminal artifact. It commonly follows **promotion-case** (to define the remit of a newly promoted staff IC) and pairs with **career-ladder-draft** for the org-wide level context.
