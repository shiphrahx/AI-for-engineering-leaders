# Manager README

## Situation

You're a new manager (or new to a team) and want to give your direct reports a head start on working with you. A manager README sets expectations, surfaces your working style, and saves everyone from figuring out your preferences through trial and error.

## The Prompt

```
You are an engineering manager writing a personal README for your direct reports.

Draft a manager README with these sections:

1. **How I work** (scheduling preferences, communication style, response time expectations, async vs. sync)
2. **What I care about** (3-4 things you consistently prioritise — be honest, not aspirational)
3. **How to get the best from me** (what kind of context, framing, or heads-up helps you help them)
4. **How I give feedback** (your approach — frequency, format, directness)
5. **What I struggle with** (1-2 genuine limitations or tendencies to watch for)
6. **Things I probably won't change** (non-negotiable working preferences they should know upfront)

Tone: first-person, direct, honest. This is a working document, not a PR exercise. Avoid anything that sounds like a mission statement.

My answers to each section:
[PASTE_YOUR_NOTES]
```

## Example Input

```
How I work: I'm an early person — best thinking before 11am. I keep afternoons for 1:1s and async. I use Slack for quick things and prefer email for anything that needs a paper trail. I aim to respond to Slack within a few hours during work hours, same day at minimum. I batch my own deep work into focus blocks and go on DND.

What I care about: Clarity over speed, honest feedback loops, building people up rather than managing them. I care a lot about team health — I think a sustainable pace makes better work than sprinting.

Getting the best from me: Tell me the context before the ask. Don't bury the lead. If you're stuck, say so early — I'd rather hear "I'm blocked" on day 2 than day 8. I'm better in writing than in impromptu conversation.

Feedback: I do feedback in 1:1s, regularly. I try to be direct and specific. I'm working on being more positive in the moment — I tend to notice problems first.

Struggles: I can get impatient when discussions go in circles without moving toward a decision. I sometimes give feedback before people have had time to think.

Won't change: No meetings before 9am or after 5pm. I take proper holidays and don't check Slack when I'm off — I expect the same from my team.
```

## Example Output

> **Working with [Name] — a README**
>
> **How I work**
> I'm sharpest in the morning. I protect time before 11am for focused work and use afternoons for 1:1s, reviews, and async catch-up. Slack is for fast things; if something needs a record, email me. I'll respond to Slack within a few hours on workdays. When I'm in a focus block I'm on DND — that's not me ignoring you, I'll be back.
>
> **What I care about**
> Clarity first — I'd rather slow down to understand the problem correctly than move fast in the wrong direction. Honest feedback loops, in both directions. Team health over team pace — I think the best work comes from people who aren't running on empty. And developing you as an engineer and a person, not just delivering the quarter's roadmap.
>
> **How to get the best from me**
> Give me context before the ask. I'm much more useful when I understand what you're solving and why, not just the surface question. If you're stuck, tell me early — the longer you wait, the less I can help. I process better in writing than in real-time conversation, so a quick Slack summary before a complex discussion helps me a lot.
>
> **How I give feedback**
> Regularly, in 1:1s, and as directly as I can. I try to be specific and actionable — "this could be better" isn't useful. Fair warning: I tend to notice what's wrong before I notice what's right. I'm working on that. If you want more positive feedback, ask — I mean it.
>
> **What I struggle with**
> Circular discussions with no movement toward a decision. I'll often push for a call or a concrete next step when I probably need to let a conversation breathe a bit longer. I also sometimes give feedback before you've had time to process — if you need space to think first, say so.
>
> **Things I won't change**
> No meetings before 9am or after 5pm. I take proper time off and don't check Slack on holiday — I encourage you to do the same. Rest is not optional.

## Tuning Notes

- **New team:** Share this before or in your first 1:1 cycle, invite them to write their own and share with you.
- **Honest revision:** Revisit this every 6-12 months — your struggles and preferences evolve, and an outdated README is worse than none.
- **Short version:** Cut to "How I work," "What I care about," and "What I struggle with" — those three carry the most signal.
