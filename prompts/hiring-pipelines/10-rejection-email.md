# Rejection Email

## Situation

A candidate went through your interview process and didn't pass. You need to tell them — respectfully, promptly, and ideally with enough useful feedback that they walk away with a positive impression of your company even though they didn't get the job.

## The Prompt

```
You are a hiring manager writing a rejection email to a candidate. Be kind, prompt, and specific enough to be useful without creating legal risk. The candidate invested their time — respect that. The best rejection emails are ones the candidate appreciates receiving.

Structure:
1. **Thank them** — specifically for their time and effort
2. **The decision** — clear, direct, not buried in the middle of the email
3. **Brief feedback** — one strength and one area for growth (optional but strongly recommended)
4. **Door open** — if you'd consider them for future roles, say so genuinely
5. **Close** — warm, professional

Candidate: [NAME]
Role: [ROLE]
Stage rejected: [WHICH_STAGE]
Key feedback: [WHAT_TO_SHARE]
Future potential: [WOULD_YOU_HIRE_THEM_LATER]
```

## Example Input

```
Candidate: Taylor
Role: Senior Backend Engineer
Stage: After technical interview (system design was strong, code review exercise was weak — missed critical bugs and feedback was vague)
Key feedback: System design thinking was strong. Code review depth needs improvement — missed correctness issues and feedback lacked specificity.
Future potential: Yes — if they strengthen their code review skills, they'd be a strong candidate.
```

## Example Output

> Subject: Your application for Senior Backend Engineer
>
> Hi Taylor,
>
> Thank you for the time you put into our interview process. I particularly enjoyed the system design discussion — your approach to structuring the webhook system was thoughtful and well-reasoned.
>
> After careful consideration, we've decided not to move forward with your application at this time. The team felt that the code review exercise didn't demonstrate the depth of analysis we look for at the senior level — particularly around identifying correctness issues and providing specific, actionable feedback.
>
> That said, your system design instincts were strong, and I could see the foundations of a great senior engineer. If you're interested, sharpening code review as a deliberate practice — focusing on correctness bugs and writing reviews that explain the "why" behind your suggestions — would make a real difference.
>
> We'd genuinely welcome a future application. If the timing feels right in 6-12 months, please reach out directly — you don't need to go through the standard application again.
>
> Wishing you the best in your search.
>
> [Your name]

## Tuning Notes

- **No feedback (legal caution):** Some companies prohibit sharing specific feedback. If so: "After reviewing all candidates, we've decided to move forward with someone whose experience more closely matches our current needs. This isn't a reflection of your abilities." It's less helpful but still respectful.
- **Early-stage rejection (resume screen):** Keep it shorter. You haven't invested enough time together to justify detailed feedback. Acknowledge receipt, communicate the decision, wish them well.
- **Strong candidate, wrong timing:** Be explicit. "You'd be a great fit for a role at [level/team] and I'd love to talk again when we have the right opening. Would it be okay to reach out when that happens?"
- **Timing:** Send within 48 hours of the decision. Every day of silence erodes the candidate's experience and your employer brand.
